The thing itself

Pornography in its current form is less than two decades old.

Pornography is not new. Humans have produced erotic images since they could produce images at all. But what people called pornography a century ago — a printed photograph passed around, a magazine purchased in shame — is to what exists today as a campfire is to a forest fire.

High-speed internet pornography — free, infinite, instantly accessible, algorithmically curated, taggable, searchable, and surfaced to you in bed at three in the morning by a device you cannot leave the house without — is a different category of stimulus than anything that came before. The human brain was not built for it. There has not been time.

And so the brain is doing what it does with any supernormal stimulus: it is adapting. The cost of that adaptation is the subject of this page.

The good news is that the same brain that adapts toward dependence also adapts away from it. The neural circuits that learned this behavior can learn another. Recovery is not a prayer. It is a system.

Dopamine, novelty, adaptation

Your brain treats novel sexual stimuli the way it treats a famine ending.

The reward system did not evolve for pleasure. It evolved for prediction error — the gap between what you expected and what you got. When the gap is positive and large, dopamine fires, and the brain learns: do that again.

A single sexual partner produces diminishing dopamine response with each repeated encounter. This is called the Coolidge effect, and it is the reason long-term monogamy is biologically interesting. Novelty resets the response. The brain treats a new partner as a new opportunity.

Internet pornography hijacks this. Each tab is a new "partner". Each click is a new prediction error. The brain, which evolved over millions of years to occasionally meet a new mate, is now meeting one every four seconds.

The receptors downregulate. The baseline shifts. What used to be exciting becomes ordinary. What used to be ordinary becomes invisible. This is not weakness. It is biology behaving exactly as predicted in a context it was never designed for.

And it is reversible. Slowly. With a protocol.
